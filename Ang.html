Autonomous driving

The subject of my talk is autonomous driving technology. I would like to start by explaining what autonomous driving is. It's a complex combination of various components that can be defined as systems where perception, decision making , and operation of the automobile are performed by electronics rather than by human input. There are several key components that I would like to focus on in this presentation.

I will be starting with the physical input needed to drive a car. Normally human controls the pedals and steering wheel to drive. Replacing that is as easy as adding actuators combined with a drive-by-wire connection to the steering. That enables electronics, with correctly programmed software, to steer and break or accelerate. For obvious reasons automatic transmission is preferred in those type of vehicles. However there were models with manual transmission in early testing with actuators that could change gears.

Another component already mentioned by me is the software needed to handle all the data. The data includes decision making which consists of planning vehicle path, trajectory and maneuvers. Second data source are the sensors which include lidar, sonar, radar and cameras to monitor the vehicle surroundings and its position. Third data source is the cloud technology. It contains high-definition maps, traffic data, algorithms for object detection, classification and decision making. It can be updated by other vehicles and let them communicate to further improve their drive ability. Another source of data are localization and mapping services, which can use the current vehicle position and maps from the cloud to help the algorithms plot best possible route to its destination.

Going back to the sensors used to gather data from the surroundings. One of them mentioned by me was lidar. What is lidar? Lidar is a surveying method that measures distance to a target by illuminating that target with a pulsed laser light, and measuring the reflected pulses with a sensor. Differences in laser return times and wavelengths can then be used to make digital 3D-representations of the target. The name lidar, is an acronym of Light Detection And Ranging. Sonar is a similar technology but instead of light it uses sound.

Now I want to talk about a real life example. To show the importance of software in autonomous driving I can point out Tesla. Tesla is an automotive company from USA. They have revolutionalised the field of electric cars and introduced autonomous driving for the masses. Whereas in normal car upgrading software won't lead to any substantial gains, Tesla in their cars can change vehicle characteristics just by upgrading their software. For example early models of Tesla S when updated gained few split seconds in their 0-60mph time, better fuel efficiency and autonomous driving upgrades. Something our 'normal' cars can just dream of.

We've looked at software, now lets talk about hardware. In our laptops most of us probably have 4 processors. In a modern Tesla car there are about 65 processors, with different computing powers and with data comparison capabilities. The processors take in data from up to 12 cameras positioned around the car, and combine it with extensive "deep learning" to teach the car to sense and interpret what is taking place around it.

What is 'deep learning' you might ask. Deep learning is a machine learning technique that teaches computers to do what comes naturally to humans: learn by example. Deep learning is a key technology behind driverless cars, enabling them to recognize a stop sign, or to distinguish a pedestrian from a lamppost. Crucial feature in a car that needs to be able to make that sort of decisions.

Moving on now to legal challenges for autonomous vehicles. The fast emergance of this technology suprised the governments, because there were no laws stating who is responsible for a self- driving car. There are also different levels of assistance from the automated systems. From lane- assist which many manufacturers now implement in their cars to full automated trucks that are being developed and tested for automated hauling of goods in the future. Imagine whole fleets of self driving trucks delivering goods and when developed nd implemented in the right way being even more safe than a real driver. For example: can a computer fall asleep at the wheel? If it has different redundancies offering it the ability to protect its systems from power failures and cloud connection problems, it would definitely be safer.

But that point brings me to another topic of concern. What happens when someone else takes control of your car? Those are the issues manufacturers will have to take into consideration. But for now governments around the world support autonomous vehicle technology. The German transport minister has proposed a bill to provide a legal framework for the use of autonomous vehicles, aiming to put fully autonomous vehicles on an equal legal footing to human drivers.

For example, in 2015 the European Commission voted in favour of legislation to require that from April 2018, all cars sold in Europe are equipped with eCall, a system that automatically contacts emergency services and directs them to the vehicle location in the event of a serious crash.This shows that the governments take new technologies seriously and in the future this could make autonomous driving required in newly produced cars.

To sum up this technology has tremendous potential and developing it will definitely be a challenging task. However in the future it could change our lives for the better.
